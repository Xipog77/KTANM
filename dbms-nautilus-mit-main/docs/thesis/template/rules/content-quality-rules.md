# Simple Rules for Writing a Method Chapter

**1. Say why before what.**
Open each section with the purpose of the component, not its contents.

**2. Give an example for every abstract idea.**
One concrete example makes an abstraction real.

**3. Justify your choices.**
Briefly explain why you did it this way and not another.

**4. Go from big picture to details.**
Show the whole system first, then zoom into each part.

**5. Define inputs and outputs of each component.**
Tell the reader what goes in, what comes out, and how parts connect.

**6. Use figures, tables, or pseudocode for anything structural.**
Don't describe in prose what a diagram shows better.

**7. Avoid long comma-lists in prose.**
Group items into categories, or move the full list to a table.

**8. Keep terminology consistent.**
One name per concept, used the same way throughout.

**9. Don't re-explain textbook material.**
Cite it and move on.

**10. After each paragraph, ask: "would the reader know why this matters?"**
If not, add one sentence that connects it back to the goal.

That's it — ten rules you can keep in mind while writing, without feeling boxed in.
